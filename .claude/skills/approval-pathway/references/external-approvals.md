# External Approvals — Parallel to Building Permit

All of these approvals are "Applicable Law" — a building permit **cannot be issued** until they are secured. They run in parallel with, but must precede, the building permit.

---

## 1. Heritage Alteration Permit

### When Required
Required before undertaking **any changes** to a property designated under the **Ontario Heritage Act**:
- Properties individually designated (**Part IV**)
- Properties within a **Heritage Conservation District (Part V)**

### What Triggers a Permit

| Trigger | Examples |
|---------|----------|
| New construction or additions | Any new building on a designated property |
| Exterior alterations | Replacing/altering windows, doors, siding, cladding, roofing, paint colour on masonry/trim |
| Hard landscaping | Installing or removing patios, porches, fences, gazebos |

**Does NOT require a permit**: General maintenance, repainting a previously painted surface (unless it alters building appearance or involves heritage attributes)

### Application Process

| Step | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Pre-consultation | Meet with municipal staff — confirms requirements and avoids processing delays |
| Submission | Scaled drawings of proposed changes to building envelope and elevations; material details; photographs of existing elevations; sometimes a Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) or Conservation Plan |
| Review | Reviewed against property's designation by-law or district guidelines; Municipal Heritage Committee often consulted |
| Decision | Municipal Council (or delegated staff) approves, approves with conditions, or refuses |

### Timelines and Appeals
- Statutory decision required within **90 days** of complete application notice
- If refused or subjected to unacceptable conditions: owner has **30 days** to appeal to the **Ontario Land Tribunal (OLT)**

## 2. Conservation Authority Permit (e.g., TRCA, Credit Valley Conservation)

### When Required
Required if property is within land **regulated by a Conservation Authority (CA)** under the Conservation Authorities Act. CA permits are **Applicable Law** — building permit cannot be issued without CA clearance.

### What Triggers a Permit
Any proposed construction, site alteration, or development within:
- Valley and stream corridors
- Wetlands
- Flood plains
- Dynamic beaches
- Waterfront

**Primary concern**: Whether work will negatively affect flooding control, erosion, pollution, or land conservation.

### Application Process
1. Submit CA permit application with supporting documentation:
   - Topographical surveys
   - Hydraulic modelling (if floodplain involvement)
   - Environmental impact studies (if natural habitats involved)
2. CA conducts site visits and coordinates with other agencies
3. Recommendations forwarded to CA's Executive Committee for approval

### Timeline Warning
If extensive environmental or wildlife studies are required, this process can take **a year or more** and may result in strict construction stipulations (e.g., restricting demolition to certain windows to protect local wildlife).

## 3. Environmental Compliance Approval (ECA)

### When Required
ECAs are required by the **Ministry of the Environment, Conservation and Parks (MECP)** for commercial, industrial, or municipal facilities with potential to impact the natural environment or human health.

### What Triggers an ECA

| Type | Trigger |
|------|---------|
| Air ECA | Emissions, noise, or vibrations from facility |
| Waste ECA | Transportation, management, processing, or disposal of waste |
| Sewage ECA | Collection, treatment, or disposal of sewage works |

### Important Notes
- ECAs are complex — intended for operations with **significant environmental impact**
- Typical process: pre-submission meetings, noise and odor assessments, Emission Summary and Dispersion Modelling
- Timeline: often **a year or more**
- **Lower-risk alternative**: Environmental Activity and Sector Registry (**EASR**) — faster and less expensive for standard emissions

**Pipeline note for residential**: ECAs are rarely triggered by standard residential development unless the project involves a sewage system (septic) or unusual noise/emission source.

## 4. Utility Connections

### 4a. Hydro / Electrical Connection

#### Process Steps
1. Determine required service size (e.g., 100A vs. 200A) via electrical load calculations
2. Request **connection design** from local utility — dictates trench specs, conduit sizes, meter base location
3. Obtain **electrical permit/notification from the Electrical Safety Authority (ESA)**
4. Complete site work: meter base, panel, trenching
5. Pass ESA inspections
6. Utility energizes the system

#### Costs
- **ESA minimum notification fee**: $88 (residential); total varies by project scope
- **Overhead service**: Less expensive (avoids excavation)
- **Underground service**: Owner pays for trenching, conduit, bedding, and site restoration — can range from low thousands to tens of thousands if rock or wet soil is involved
- **Basic Connection allowance**: Utilities (e.g., Hydro One) typically provide up to **30 metres of overhead wire** for 200A service; distances beyond this or system expansions (new poles, transformers) trigger **project-specific cost contributions** paid by the owner

### 4b. Water and Wastewater (Municipal Systems)

#### Approvals Required
In regions with a **two-tier municipal system** (e.g., Peel Region), owners must secure:
- Written approval from the **upper-tier regional government** for water, wastewater, and storm sewer connections
- This approval is required **before** the lower-tier municipality will issue the building permit

#### Process
1. Submit site servicing drawings
2. If required: submit **Water and Wastewater demand table** for capacity modelling
3. If adjacent to a regionally maintained road: also submit stormwater management report and grading drawing
4. Owner hires independent contractors (region does not provide construction services)
5. Contractors must:
   - Obtain **underground utility locates** via Ontario One Call before digging
   - Follow OBC and regional standards
6. Municipality collects **securities** (financial guarantees) to ensure works are completed to standard

#### Capacity Warning
Water and wastewater capacity is **finite** and can cause project delays. Under **Bill 60 (2025)**, municipalities can reallocate capacity from stalled developments to shovel-ready projects ("use it or lose it"). Securing servicing capacity early is critical on infill development.

## Applicable Law — Sequencing Summary

The building permit application can be submitted concurrently with external approval applications, but **must not be issued** until all applicable law approvals are in hand.

| External Approval | Typical Lead Time | Applicable Law? |
|------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Heritage Alteration Permit | 30–90 days | Yes |
| Conservation Authority Permit | 2 months – 12+ months | Yes |
| Environmental Compliance Approval | 6–18+ months | Yes (for ECA-required uses) |
| Site Plan Agreement | 3–12 months | Yes |
| Minor Variance / CoA | 2–5 months | Yes |
| ESA Electrical Notification | Days–weeks | Yes |
| Regional Water/Wastewater Approval | 1–3 months | Yes |
